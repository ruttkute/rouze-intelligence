You need to know what customers actually want

Common situation:

Your product team has a roadmap, but it's based on assumptions. Sales says customers want Feature X. Support says they're complaining about Feature Y. Your competitor just launched something completely different. Who's right?

Traditional approach:

  • Commission market research study (3-6 months, €15K-50K)
  • Survey your existing customers (biased sample, 40% response rate)
  • Read analyst reports (generic, same data your competitors have)

Problem: By the time you have answers, the market has moved.

How we find out what customers actually want

01

Listen where they actually talk

We analyze unfiltered conversations happening right now in:

  • Product review sites (1,000+ reviews per analysis)
  • Industry forums (Reddit, specialized communities)
  • Support tickets (public-facing patterns)
  • Social media discussions (not promotional posts)
  • Job postings (what skills are companies hiring for?)

These people aren't talking to YOU—they're talking to each other. That means you get honest opinions, not survey-friendly answers.

02

Identify patterns, not opinions

We don't just count mentions. We look for:

Pain points What problems come up repeatedly?
Workarounds What are people cobbling together?
Unmet needs "I wish there was..." statements
Sentiment shifts What's frustration increasing about?
03

Validate with cross-source confirmation

We don't trust a single data point. A complaint is just noise. But when the same issue appears across Reddit, G2, support forums, AND professional communities—that's a signal.

Example:

"Mobile performance" complaints appeared in:
→ 18% of App Store reviews (n=847)
→ 43% of Reddit threads about the category
→ 29% of G2 "areas for improvement"
→ 12% increase in competitor mentions with "fast" descriptor

Result: Strong signal that mobile performance is a competitive vulnerability.

How this worked for a pharmaceutical company

Situation

Physician recommendations declining for 3 months. Sales blamed pricing. Marketing blamed competition. Product team blamed doctors "not understanding the benefits."

What we found (in 6 days)

Analysis of medical forums and review sites revealed physicians weren't confused about benefits—they were frustrated with complex dosing instructions that increased their liability risk.

Cross-validated across:
→ Physician discussion forums (325 mentions)
→ Medical device review sites (18% of negative feedback)
→ Professional LinkedIn groups (recurring theme)

What they did

Simplified packaging with clearer dosing charts. Updated physician training materials to address liability concerns.

Outcome

Physician sentiment improved 34% within 60 days
Saved €480,000 in reactive costs (label changes, training, damage control)
Got the real answer in 6 days vs. 3-month research study
